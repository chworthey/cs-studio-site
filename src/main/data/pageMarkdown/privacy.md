## Teaching Moment

It might seem interesting to you that I choose to not prompt you for cookie settings and don't follow the usual wall of text.

So why does everybody else do it? Well...

The most common reason is that they don't know themselves and everybody else does it, so they do it to look more "official". They use website builders and things of the sort that add those features for them, and website users get accustomed to those features. But, once your company gets to a certain size (large), that's when these things actually become important.

Let's discuss some law! Disclaimer: I'm not a legal expert.

## GDPR & State Legislation

GDPR stands for *General Data Protection Regulation*, and it's a regulation that particularly protects people in the EU. Here are the rights it promises:
1. The right to be informed
2. The right of access
3. The right to rectification
4. The right to erasure
5. The right to restrict processing
6. The right to data portability
7. The right to object
8. Rights in relation to automated decision making and profiling

It boils down to these principles:
- You need to be informed about data collection
- You need to be able to request a copy of your data and understand how it is being processed
- You can correct inaccurate personal data about yourself
- You can request deletion of your data, you can be forgotten about
- The copy of the data you receive must be structured/machine-readable
- You may refuse the collection of your personal data
- You have the right to not be subject to adverse automated decision making if it significantly affects you under some circumstances

At the same time, this is just in the EU. Right? Well... Not anymore.

Protections in the EU tend to precede protections elsewhere, so large US-based companies began to provide the same rights to US users even though, at the time, there were no such protections in the US. I applauded this, it seemed ethical.

But then this happened: after the large companies started implementing this, users got used to it. At that point, it became all about the optics: "Does our site have that thing yet? No? Well that makes us look bad!"

But, because the smaller companies didn't have the resources to implement it themselves, they outsourced it, and that's how we got it everywhere.

With privacy protections entering the public consciousness, states began to look at privacy laws to follow in EU's footsteps.
